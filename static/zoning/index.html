sf zoning map

in san francisco, what you can build and where depends on a complex system of zoning and height restrictions.

many of our beautiful neighborhoods and iconic buildings don't meet later zoning laws, meaning that they couldn't be built today.

here's our current zoning. you can drag the map. hold shift to rotate. pinch or scroll to zoom. click for details.

do want to upzone the red parts of the map? want a more inclusive, urban city to call home? join sf yimby

RH-1: the burbs

RH-1(D) RH-1 RH-1(S)

much of the residential land in our city is suburban-style development where you're only allowed to build a single unit per lot. RH-1(D) means those units have to be detached houses.

RH-2 and -3: low density residential

RH-2 RH-3

these zones are better than RH-1, but still pretty sparse, and still car centered.

RM: residential mixed

RM-1 RM-2 RM-3 RM-4 RC-3 RC-4 RCD RTO RTO-M

these zones allow housing, grocery stores, office space, and shops in the same neighborhood. that makes them friendlier for people who want to walk or bike.

MUO MUG MUR UMU WMUG WMUO SSO

soma / fidi has it's own zones, including three kinds of "mixed-use urban" and one kind of "urban mixed use". the bureaucracy has no chill.

RED: dense residential

RED RED-MX RH DTR SB-DTR TB DTR

soma has "residential enclaves", which are several-story apartment complexes. fidi has "downtown residential", which contains skyscrapers.

NCT and NCD: "neighborhood commercial"

NC-1 NC-2 NC-3 NC-S NCD NCT NCT-1 NCT-2 NCT-3

these are street districts that allow housing, shops, and transit. they vary in density. some are pretty great.

C: commercial

C-2 C-3-G C-3-O C-3-O(SD) C-3-R C-3-S

this mostly means office towers, but also includes shopping malls. no housing.

M and PDR: industrial areas

M-1 M-2 PDR-1-B PDR-1-D PDR-1-G PDR-2 SALI SLI

M-1 and M-2 are light and heavy industrial. PDR stands for "production, distribution, repair" and includes things like warehouses and auto body shops. they're fine on the outskirts of the city, but it's hard to justify warehouses and expanses of concrete in neighborhoods like SoMa where a studio can easily cost $3,000 a month.

special zones

CCB CRNC CVR

MB-O MB-OS MB-RA HP-RA SPD

PM-OS PM-S PM-CF PM-MU1 PM-MU2 PM-R

some areas, like Chinatown and South Park, get their own special zoning. the rules vary, but for the most part these areas are great. unlike the bedroom community garage-and-driveway vibe of RH-1, special zones are mixed use and have a unique local character.
