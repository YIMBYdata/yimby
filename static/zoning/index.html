zoning map

in san francisco, what you can build and where depends on a complex system of zoning and height restrictions.

our most beautiful and iconic buildings are often in violation of current zoning, meaning that they couldn't be built today.

here's where we are today.

RH-1: the burbs

much of the residential land in our city is suburban-style development where you're only allowed to build a single unit per lot. RH-1(D) means those units have to be detached houses.

RH-2 and -3: low density residential

these zones are better than RH-1, but still pretty sparse, and still car centered.

RM: residential mixed

these zones allow housing, grocery stores, office space, and shops in the same neighborhood. that makes them friendlier for people who want to walk or bike.

NCT and NCD: "neighborhood commercial"

these are mixed districts that allow housing, shops, and transit. they vary in density, but some are pretty great.

C: commercial

this mostly means office towers, but also includes shopping malls. unfortunately in SF, most commercial development has no convenient transit. people are expected to drive.

M and PDR: industrial areas

M-1 and M-2 are light and heavy industrial. PDR stands for "production, distribution, repair" and includes things like warehouses and auto body shops. they're fine on the outskirts of the city, but it makes no sense to have vast warehouses and expanses of concrete right next to neighborhoods like SoMa and FiDi where a studio can easily cost $3,000 a month.

special zones

areas like Chinatown and South Park get their own, special zoning. the rules vary a lot, but for the most part these areas are great. unlike the undifferentiated, bedroom community garage-and-driveway vibe of RH-1, special zones are mostly mixed use and have a unique local character.
